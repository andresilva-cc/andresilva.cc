# Plain Language

**Read on-demand when the task involves writing or rewriting text that must be understood by a broad public audience — legal, regulatory, policy, terms-of-service, government, healthcare, financial, or instructional text — or when translating such text.**

Plain language is communication that puts readers first: readers can find what they need, understand it, and use it. It is not "dumbing down" — it is doing the hard work so the reader does less. General writing craft is in `copy-craft.md`; this file covers the techniques specific to high-stakes, broad-audience text.

> **Toolkit-managed file — do not edit per-project.** These rules are static and shared across every project using the toolkit. Project-specific decisions belong in the project's own decisions doc — never here.

## The standard

### Rule: Judge success by reader use, not by a formula score
**Applies to:** All plain-language work.
**Why:** The international definition (ISO 24495-1:2023) measures plain language by whether content is *relevant* to readers and whether readers can *find, understand, and use* it — not by a readability score. Readability formulas are a useful sanity check, never the goal. A text can score well and still be unusable; test with real readers when stakes are high.

### Rule: Identify the reader and what they need to do before writing
**Applies to:** Any plain-language rewrite or original.
**Why:** Plain language is reader-relative — "common words" depends on who the reader is. Write for the actual audience (a benefits claimant, a patient, a taxpayer), not for a colleague or a lawyer. Lead with what the reader needs to do or decide.

## Sentences

### Rule: Keep sentences short — one idea each
**Numeric baseline:** Average 15–20 words; hard ceiling around 25. Split anything longer.
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** Long sentences force the reader to hold multiple clauses in working memory. Brazil's Política Nacional de Linguagem Simples (Lei nº 15.263/2025) and the US Federal Plain Language Guidelines both name short sentences as a core requirement. One idea per sentence; if you find an "and", "which", or semicolon joining two ideas, split it.

### Rule: Use direct subject–verb–object order
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** Front-loading subordinate clauses and conditions ("Notwithstanding the foregoing, and subject to Section 4, the applicant shall...") makes the reader wait for the main point. Put the actor first, then the action, then the object. State the general rule before the exceptions.

### Rule: Write in the active voice
**Applies to:** All plain-language text; obligations and instructions especially.
**Why:** Active voice names who must do what — "You must file by April 15" beats "Filing must be completed by April 15". Passive voice hides the actor, which in legal and policy text creates real ambiguity about responsibility. Both the US guidelines and Lei nº 15.263/2025 mandate active voice.

### Rule: State requirements as direct instructions, addressing the reader
**Applies to:** Procedures, obligations, eligibility, forms.
**Why:** "You must", "send us", "you can" is clearer than third-person abstraction ("the applicant is required to"). Personal pronouns and the imperative tell the reader exactly what applies to them and what to do.

## Words

### Rule: Use common everyday words; reserve technical terms for when they're necessary
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** "Use" not "utilize", "buy" not "purchase", "end" not "terminate", "about" not "with respect to", "help" not "assistance". Latinate and bureaucratic diction signals officialdom, not precision. Keep a technical term only when it is genuinely the precise word and no common word will do.

### Rule: Define every technical, legal, or domain term on first use
**Applies to:** Any term the target reader is unlikely to know.
**Why:** A necessary technical term is acceptable; an undefined one is not. Define it in-line the first time it appears, in plain words — not by cross-referencing a definitions section the reader must hunt down. Lei nº 15.263/2025 explicitly requires explaining technical terms.

### Rule: Avoid uncommon foreign words and untranslated Latin
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** "Inter alia", "prima facie", "per se", "vis-à-vis", "ergo" exclude readers who don't know them and add nothing a common word can't carry. Use the everyday equivalent ("among other things", "on its face").

### Rule: Cut legal and bureaucratic doublets, archaisms, and filler
**Applies to:** Legal, policy, and terms text especially.
**Why:** "Cease and desist", "null and void", "due to the fact that", "in the event that", "herein", "thereof", "aforementioned", "pursuant to" add length and formality without meaning. "Stop", "void", "because", "if", "in this", "of it", "under" carry the same content.

### Rule: Use the same word for the same thing throughout
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** In ordinary prose, synonym variety is fine; in legal and instructional text it is a defect. If "the form" and "the application" and "the document" all mean the same object, the reader cannot be sure — pick one term and repeat it.

## Structure

### Rule: Break dense prose into lists
**Applies to:** Any sentence enumerating three or more conditions, steps, or items.
**Why:** A list lets the reader see the items as discrete and count them; the same content as a comma-spliced sentence forces parsing. Use a numbered list for sequence or priority, a bulleted list otherwise. Keep list items parallel in grammar.

### Rule: Structure for scanning — informative headings, short sections, white space
**Applies to:** Documents longer than a few paragraphs.
**Why:** Readers locate what they need by scanning headings. Use question-form or task-form headings the reader would actually search for ("How do I appeal?" not "Appeals"). Keep paragraphs short and one-topic; use tables for if/then conditions; lead each section with its key point.

### Rule: Prioritize essential information; put it first
**Applies to:** All plain-language text.
**Why:** Lei nº 15.263/2025 names prioritization of essential information as a requirement. Readers of legal and government text are usually looking for one thing — eligibility, a deadline, an amount, an action. Put it where the reader looks first, not after pages of preamble.

## Readability checks

### Rule: Use a readability formula as a flag, not a target
**Numeric baseline:** Flesch Reading Ease ≥ 60 (plain, ~8th–9th grade) for a general public audience; Flesch–Kincaid Grade Level ≤ 8 for broad consumer text. Lower the bar further for vulnerable or low-literacy audiences.
**Applies to:** Public-facing documents.
**Why:** Formulas score sentence length and syllable count — proxies, not comprehension. A passing score with undefined jargon is still unusable; a failing score may flag genuinely necessary long sentences. Use the number to *find* problem passages, then fix them by the rules above. Verify with real readers when the stakes are high.

### Rule: Read it aloud and test the rewrite against the original meaning
**Applies to:** Every plain-language rewrite, translation especially.
**Why:** Reading aloud surfaces sentences that stall. And simplification must not change the legal or factual content — a plain rewrite that drops a condition or softens an obligation is wrong, not plain. Check the rewrite preserves every requirement, exception, and right of the source.
